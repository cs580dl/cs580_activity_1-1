# Grading & Feedback Checklist for 1-1 Discussion: Ethics and AI

## About This Checklist

### Purpose and Use

This checklist is a supplement to the *Graduate Discussion Rubric* (G&R) and the *1-1 Discussion: Ethics and AI* discussion prompt (DP). It is designed to help students and graders understand the specific expectations for each criterion and proficiency level in this discussion. It translates the general discussion-rubric descriptors into specific observable requirements for the initial post, response posts, discussion timing, ethical reasoning, communication quality, and citation or attribution practices.

Students should use this checklist before submitting the discussion to self-assess whether their work addresses each requirement. After the discussion is graded, students should use the instructor-completed checklist to review feedback, identify patterns in their strengths and opportunities for improvement, and apply that feedback to future CS 580 discussions and activities. Each criterion section includes a narrative feedback template and requirement details for exceeds expectations (EE), meets expectations (ME), partially meets expectations (PM), and does not meet expectations (DN).

## 1. Comprehension Feedback

This criterion focuses on the quality and development of the **initial post**. To meet expectations, students should develop an initial post with a clear point of view or idea, appropriate detail, and direct responses to the discussion prompt.

To exceed expectations, students should present an especially organized and clear point of view supported by rich, significant detail that connects personal or professional experience, field-of-study context, and ethical analysis of AI in a meaningful way.


### Narrative Feedback for Comprehension

<Summary sentence identifying the student’s overall proficiency level for Comprehension and briefly explaining whether the submission shows no initial post or no organized point of view (DN), a discernible [graduate-level attempt](#graduate-level-attempt) with gaps in organization or detail (PM), a complete initial post with an identifiable point of view and appropriate detail (ME), or an especially organized, clear, and richly detailed initial post (EE).>

**Strengths:** <One paragraph describing specific strengths in the student’s initial post. Reference observable evidence such as the self-introduction, AI tools or deep learning technologies mentioned, explanation of AI’s influence on the student’s field of study, identification of ethical concerns, and the clarity or depth of the student’s point of view.>

**Opportunities:** <One paragraph describing 1–2 specific improvements that would help the student reach the next proficiency level for Comprehension. For DN, focus on developing an actual initial post with an identifiable point of view. For PM, focus on completing all required prompt elements and adding enough detail to explain the main idea. For ME, focus on adding EE-level richness, such as more specific examples, stronger integration of personal or professional context, clearer comparison of ethical concerns, or fuller development of why the chosen concern matters. For submissions that meet all EE requirements, focus on refinement, precision, or continued depth of ethical analysis.>

**Resources:** <One paragraph identifying 1–2 resources that would help the student improve Comprehension in discussion posts. Recommended resources may include the discussion prompt, the Graduate Discussion Rubric, the annotated bibliography, course examples, or writing resources that support stronger paragraph development and detail.>

### Requirements Checklist for Comprehension


#### DN for Comprehension (0%)

A submission is DN for this criterion when it does not meet the PM requirement below, such as when no initial post is developed or the post does not present an organized point of view or idea.


#### PM for Comprehension (70%)

*Develops an initial post with a point of view or idea but with some gaps in organization and detail*


To partially meet expectations (PM) for this criterion, the submission must address the following requirement:

- [ ] [1.1.1.](#111) Submission makes a discernible [graduate-level attempt](#graduate-level-attempt) to develop the initial post around a point of view or idea about AI use and ethics.


#### ME for Comprehension (90%)

*Develops an initial post with a point of view or idea using appropriate detail*


To meet expectations (ME) for this criterion, the submission must meet the above requirement and address all of the following requirements:

- [ ] [1.2.1.](#121) Submission introduces the student in a course-appropriate way in the initial post.
- [ ] [1.2.2.](#122) Submission identifies examples of AI tools or deep learning technologies the student currently uses in personal or professional life.
- [ ] [1.2.3.](#123) Submission explains how AI has influenced the student’s field of study so far.
- [ ] [1.2.4.](#124) Submission identifies at least one ethical concern the student considers especially important when adopting or using AI technologies.
- [ ] [1.2.5.](#125) Submission explains why the identified ethical concern matters using appropriate detail and an identifiable point of view.


#### EE for Comprehension (100%)

*Develops an initial post with an organized, clear point of view or idea using rich and significant detail*


To exceed expectations (EE), the submission must meet all above requirements and address at least three (3) of the following requirements:

- [ ] [1.3.1.](#131) Submission connects the chosen ethical concern to a specific AI lifecycle stage, system behavior, stakeholder group, or real-world use context.
- [ ] [1.3.2.](#132) Submission uses rich, specific detail from personal, professional, academic, or observed AI use rather than relying mainly on generic statements.
- [ ] [1.3.3.](#133) Submission explains why the chosen ethical concern is especially critical by discussing tradeoffs, consequences, or competing priorities.
- [ ] [1.3.4.](#134) Submission connects the student’s field of study, goals, or professional context to the ethical concern in a concrete way.
- [ ] [1.3.5.](#135) Submission integrates relevant course ideas or credible outside context to deepen the initial post’s point of view.
- [ ] [1.3.6.](#136) Submission organizes the initial post so the introduction, examples, and ethical stance work together as a coherent whole.


## 2. Timeliness Feedback

This criterion focuses on when the **initial post** is submitted. For this Module One discussion, the official on-time deadline for the initial post is **Thursday at 11:59 p.m. Eastern**.

- [ ] ME (100%): Initial post is submitted on time, by Thursday at 11:59 p.m. Eastern.
- [ ] PM (70%): Initial post is submitted one day late, no later than Friday at 11:59 p.m. Eastern.
- [ ] DN (0%): Initial post is submitted two or more days late, or not at all.

To exceed expectations, students should provide relevant and meaningful response posts that clarify, extend, or deepen the discussion with thoughtful explanation and detail rather than generic agreement or disagreement.

Reference observable evidence such as replies to classmates outside the student’s own thread, relevance to peer ideas, explanation of how concerns may evolve, reflection on human responsibility, clarifying detail, follow-up questions, or respectful dialogue moves.>

**Opportunities:** <One paragraph describing 1–2 specific improvements that would help the student reach the next proficiency level for Engagement. For DN, focus on moving beyond generic comments by responding directly to what peers actually said. For PM, focus on making each reply more clearly relevant and more fully explained. For ME, focus on adding EE-level depth, such as clarifying a peer’s idea, extending it with evidence or examples, or using reply posts to move the conversation forward. For submissions that meet all EE requirements, focus on continued sophistication, synthesis, or peer-to-peer scholarly dialogue.>

**Resources:** <One paragraph identifying 1–2 resources that would help the student improve Engagement in discussion replies. Recommended resources may include the response-post directions in the prompt, the Graduate Discussion Rubric, the annotated bibliography, and writing resources on constructive discussion-board responses.>

## 4. Critical Thinking Feedback

This criterion focuses on the quality of the conclusions and reasoning shown across the **initial post and response posts**. To meet expectations, students should draw informed conclusions that are justified with evidence.

To exceed expectations, students should draw especially insightful conclusions that are thoroughly defended with evidence and examples, showing depth of reasoning about AI ethics, evolving risks, and human responsibility.


### Narrative Feedback for Critical Thinking

<Summary sentence identifying the student’s overall proficiency level for Critical Thinking and briefly explaining whether the submission does not draw logical conclusions (DN), draws logical conclusions with limited support (PM), draws informed conclusions justified with evidence (ME), or draws insightful conclusions thoroughly defended with evidence and examples (EE).>

**Strengths:** <One paragraph describing specific strengths in the student’s critical thinking across the discussion. Reference observable evidence such as defensible conclusions about important ethical concerns, reasons or examples used to justify those conclusions, links to peer ideas, course concepts, personal or professional evidence, or thoughtful analysis of human responsibility in AI development.>

**Opportunities:** <One paragraph describing 1–2 specific improvements that would help the student reach the next proficiency level for Critical Thinking. For DN, focus on making explicit logical conclusions rather than only listing ideas. For PM, focus on justifying conclusions with clearer reasons, evidence, or examples. For ME, focus on adding EE-level insight, such as stronger synthesis, attention to tradeoffs, fuller defense of claims, or deeper analysis of how ethical concerns evolve as AI capabilities expand. For submissions that meet all EE requirements, focus on continued nuance, depth, and transfer of ethical reasoning to future course topics.>

**Resources:** <One paragraph identifying 1–2 resources that would help the student improve Critical Thinking. Recommended resources may include the discussion prompt, the Graduate Discussion Rubric, the annotated bibliography, and support resources on analysis, argumentation, and evidence-based reasoning.>

<Motivational, forward-looking closing sentence encouraging the student to continue using ethical reasoning, evidence, and reflection to make stronger conclusions about AI systems and the humans who build and use them.>


### Requirements Checklist for Critical Thinking


#### DN for Critical Thinking (0%)

A submission is DN for this criterion when it does not draw logical conclusions about the ethical issues under discussion.


#### PM for Critical Thinking (70%)

*Draws logical conclusions*


To partially meet expectations (PM) for this criterion, the submission must address the following requirement:

- [ ] [4.1.1.](#411) Submission makes a discernible [graduate-level attempt](#graduate-level-attempt) to draw logical conclusions about AI ethics, evolving concerns, or human responsibility.


#### ME for Critical Thinking (90%)

*Draws informed conclusions that are justified with evidence*


To meet expectations (ME) for this criterion, the submission must meet the above requirement and address all of the following requirements:

- [ ] [4.2.1.](#421) Submission states a clear conclusion or position in the initial post about which ethical concern or concerns are most critical.
- [ ] [4.2.2.](#422) Submission justifies the initial-post conclusion with relevant reasons, examples, evidence, or experience.
- [ ] [4.2.3.](#423) Submission draws logical conclusions in the response posts about how ethical concerns may evolve and/or what human responsibility in AI development requires.
- [ ] [4.2.4.](#424) Submission justifies response-post conclusions with relevant reasons, examples, evidence, or discussion-specific detail.
- [ ] [4.2.5.](#425) Across the posts, submission connects conclusions to concrete impacts on users, developers, institutions, or society.


#### EE for Critical Thinking (100%)

*Draws insightful conclusions that are thoroughly defended with evidence and examples*


To exceed expectations (EE), the submission must meet all above requirements and address at least three (3) of the following requirements:

- [ ] [4.3.1.](#431) Submission thoroughly defends key conclusions with multiple forms of evidence or examples across the initial and response posts.
- [ ] [4.3.2.](#432) Submission considers counterarguments, tradeoffs, limitations, or competing ethical priorities when defending conclusions.
- [ ] [4.3.3.](#433) Submission connects an ethical concern to specific technical or lifecycle mechanisms such as data, model behavior, deployment, governance, interface design, or monitoring.
- [ ] [4.3.4.](#434) Submission synthesizes peer ideas, course concepts, and/or credible sources to deepen the discussion rather than treating them as separate points.
- [ ] [4.3.5.](#435) Submission explains how human responsibility is distributed across multiple stakeholders rather than treating the AI system as an independent moral agent.
- [ ] [4.3.6.](#436) Submission shows insight into how ethical concerns may change as AI becomes more capable, more autonomous, or more widely adopted.
- [ ] [4.3.7.](#437) Submission distinguishes between closely related concerns, such as transparency versus accountability or bias versus fairness, to sharpen the reasoning.

## 6. Citations and Attributions Feedback

Citations and Attributions is **not included in this discussion grade**. It is provided as formative guidance because the Graduate Discussion Rubric directs students to use proper citation methods when referencing sources and to acknowledge any generative AI use.

This feedback focuses on how clearly and consistently the discussion posts credit [borrowed ideas](#borrowed-idea), outside sources, and any AI-supported assistance. To meet expectations, students should make borrowed material traceable and acknowledge AI use when it supported the work. APA 7 perfection is **not** required for ME, but the reader should be able to find the source and understand what was borrowed or assisted.


### Narrative Feedback for Citations and Attributions

<Summary sentence identifying the student’s overall citation and attribution level and briefly explaining whether borrowed ideas or AI use requiring acknowledgement are largely uncited or unacknowledged (DN), mostly attributed but with major errors (PM), clearly and findably attributed with only minor errors (ME), or accurately, consistently, and professionally attributed with few or no minor errors (EE).>

**Strengths:** <One paragraph describing specific strengths in the student’s citation and attribution practices across the discussion posts. Reference observable evidence such as in-text citations, clear source names, usable URLs or reference entries, acknowledgement of AI assistance, or consistent matching between borrowed ideas and sources.>

**Opportunities:** <One paragraph describing 1–2 specific improvements that would help the student strengthen citation and attribution habits. For DN, focus on adding clear attribution whenever outside ideas or AI support are used. For PM, focus on fixing major problems such as missing citations, missing source information, or missing AI acknowledgement. For ME, focus on improving consistency, completeness, and APA 7 alignment. For EE, focus on continued polish and professional integration of source use in discussion writing.>

**Resources:** <One paragraph identifying 1–2 resources that would help the student improve citations and attributions. Recommended resources may include the Student GenAI Guide, the Shapiro Library citation guide for AI tools, APA 7 support resources, and library or writing-center research support.>

<Motivational, forward-looking closing sentence reminding the student that strong attribution supports academic integrity, ethical AI use, and more credible discussion writing even when this criterion is feedback-only.>

## A. Requirement Details


### 1. Comprehension Requirements


#### 0. DN for Comprehension (0%)


A submission is DN for this criterion when the following condition applies:

- <a id="101"></a>1.0.1. Submission does not develop an initial post with an organized point of view or idea.
  - **GO**: The initial post is missing, is so undeveloped that no point of view or idea can be identified, or does not address the discussion in a meaningful way.
  - **NOGO**: The submission shows at least a discernible graduate-level attempt to develop an initial post with a point of view or idea.
  - **Source**: G&R, Rubric, Comprehension; DP, Initial Post.
  - ***Notes***: Use DN when the student does not actually develop the initial post as a discussion contribution. A few isolated phrases, copied prompt language, or unrelated commentary are not enough.


#### 1. PM for Comprehension (70%)


To partially meet expectations (PM) for this criterion, the submission must address the following requirement:

- <a id="111"></a>1.1.1. Submission makes a discernible [graduate-level attempt](#graduate-level-attempt) to develop the initial post around a point of view or idea about AI use and ethics.
  - **GO**: The submission includes an initial post that makes a meaningful attempt to express a point of view or idea about the student’s use of AI, the influence of AI on the student’s field of study, and/or important ethical concerns in AI adoption or use.
  - **NOGO**: The submission is blank, unrelated, limited to a greeting or placeholder, or does not show a meaningful attempt to address the discussion prompt.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, Rubric, Comprehension; DP, Initial Post.
  - ***Notes***: For PM, the attempt may still have gaps in organization, prompt coverage, or detail. The key issue is that the student is clearly trying to address the initial-post task at a graduate level.


#### 2. ME for Comprehension (90%)


To meet expectations (ME) for this criterion, the submission must meet the above requirement and address all of the following requirements:

- <a id="121"></a>1.2.1. Submission introduces the student in a course-appropriate way in the initial post.
  - **GO**: The initial post includes a brief self-introduction appropriate for the course discussion board, such as preferred name, background, interests, location, or professional experience, to the extent the student is comfortable sharing.
  - **NOGO**: The initial post omits the introduction entirely or includes no identifiable self-introductory content.
  - **Source**: DP, Initial Post.
  - ***Notes***: Do not require any specific personal details beyond what the prompt reasonably invites. The student may choose what to disclose.

  - **GO**: Wording, paragraphing, and transitions are clear enough that they support understanding rather than obscure it.

  - **Source**: G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication.
  - ***Notes***: Minor grammar or style issues do not block ME unless they affect understanding.
- <a id="525"></a>5.2.5. Submission directly addresses the prompt and clearly signals when the student is responding to a peer’s idea.
  - **GO**: The posts directly address the discussion prompt and clearly signal when the student is responding to a peer’s idea.

  - **Source**: DP, Initial Post and Response Posts; G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication.
  - ***Notes***: This requirement operationalizes audience awareness in a discussion-board setting.

  - ***Notes***: Precise language should clarify, not merely add jargon.
- <a id="533"></a>5.3.3. Submission integrates claims, examples, and evidence into a coherent discussion narrative that promotes thorough understanding.
  - **GO**: Claims, examples, and evidence are integrated into a coherent discussion narrative rather than appearing as disconnected pieces.
  - **NOGO**: The posts include claims and examples but do not connect them smoothly enough to promote thorough understanding.
  - **Source**: G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication.
  - ***Notes***: This requirement rewards communication quality, not just the presence of evidence.
- <a id="534"></a>5.3.4. Submission uses concise, polished prose that improves readability without sounding formulaic or superficial.
  - **GO**: The prose is concise and polished enough that readers can focus on the ideas rather than on wording or flow problems.
  - **NOGO**: The writing is generally understandable but still contains enough wordiness, awkwardness, or sentence-level issues to limit polish.
  - **Source**: G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication.
  - ***Notes***: Plain, direct prose can earn EE when it is intentionally polished.
- <a id="535"></a>5.3.5. Submission anticipates reader needs by clarifying context, assumptions, or limits of the student’s claims.
  - **GO**: The student anticipates reader needs by clarifying assumptions, background, or limits of the claim where doing so helps understanding.
  - **NOGO**: The writing communicates the main point but leaves important assumptions or contextual limits unexplained.
  - **Source**: G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication.
  - ***Notes***: This is especially valuable when discussing complex or evolving ethical concerns.
- <a id="536"></a>5.3.6. Submission uses especially constructive, collegial response language that advances scholarly dialogue with peers.
  - **GO**: The response posts use especially constructive, collegial language that supports scholarly dialogue and peer learning.
  - **NOGO**: The response posts are respectful enough for ME but do not show especially strong dialogue-building communication.
  - **Source**: G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication; DP, Response Posts.
  - ***Notes***: This requirement recognizes discussion-specific excellence in peer communication.
- <a id="537"></a>5.3.7. Submission integrates source attribution smoothly when outside ideas or AI support are used.
  - **GO**: When outside ideas or AI support are used, attribution is integrated smoothly so the reader can track the source without losing the flow of the discussion.
  - **NOGO**: Attribution is missing, unclear, or inserted in a way that distracts from understanding.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage; G&R, Rubric, Clear Communication.
  - ***Notes***: This overlaps with formative C&A guidance, but here the focus is readability and clarity.

### 6. Citations and Attributions Requirements


#### 0. DN for Citations and Attributions (0%)

*Formative only; not included in the activity total*


A submission is DN for this guidance when the following condition applies:

- <a id="601"></a>6.0.1. Borrowed ideas or AI-supported content that require attribution are largely uncited, unacknowledged, or not traceable to a source.
  - **GO**: Borrowed ideas or AI-supported content that require attribution are largely uncited, unacknowledged, or not traceable to a source.
  - **NOGO**: Most borrowed ideas are attributed in a way that allows the reader to trace them, and any AI-supported content requiring acknowledgement is acknowledged.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage.
  - ***Notes***: Because this section is formative, use DN language to describe the issue clearly without affecting the activity total.


#### 1. PM for Citations and Attributions (70%)

*Uses citations for ideas requiring attribution, with major errors*


To partially meet expectations (PM) for this guidance, the submission must address the following requirement:

- <a id="611"></a>6.1.1. Most [borrowed ideas](#borrowed-idea) include a citation, acknowledgement, and/or corresponding source information, but major citation or attribution errors may remain.
  - **GO**: Most borrowed ideas include a citation, acknowledgement, and/or corresponding source information, but one or more major attribution errors remain, such as missing source information, unclear linkage between idea and source, or missing AI acknowledgement when AI support was used.
  - **NOGO**: Many borrowed ideas are not attributed at all, or attribution problems are so extensive that the sources cannot be found or the AI support cannot be understood.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage; academic integrity expectations for graduate-level discussion writing.
  - ***Notes***: For PM, the student is trying to attribute sources, but major problems still remain.


#### 2. ME for Citations and Attributions (90%)

*Uses citations for ideas requiring attribution, with consistent minor errors*


To meet expectations (ME) for this guidance, the submission must meet the above requirement and address all of the following requirements:

- <a id="621"></a>6.2.1. All borrowed ideas include a citation, acknowledgement, and/or source information that allows the reader to find the source.
  - **GO**: Every borrowed idea used in the discussion posts includes enough citation, acknowledgement, link, or source information for the reader to find the source.
  - **NOGO**: Some borrowed ideas are not attributed, or the information provided is too incomplete for the reader to find the source.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; academic integrity expectations for graduate-level discussion writing.
  - ***Notes***: ME does not require perfect APA 7 formatting. Findability and clarity matter most.
- <a id="622"></a>6.2.2. Any generative AI use that supported the work is acknowledged clearly enough for the instructor to understand how it was used.
  - **GO**: If generative AI tools supported the work, the student acknowledges that use clearly enough for the instructor to understand how AI supported the discussion writing or idea development.
  - **NOGO**: AI support appears to have been used but is not acknowledged, or the acknowledgement is too vague to understand the role AI played.
  - **Source**: G&R, AI Usage.
  - ***Notes***: If no AI was used, this requirement is satisfied by the absence of AI-dependent content requiring acknowledgement.
- <a id="623"></a>6.2.3. Citation and attribution errors are minor rather than major.
  - **GO**: Any remaining citation or attribution problems are minor style-level issues rather than major errors that interfere with source tracing or academic integrity.
  - **NOGO**: Major errors remain, such as missing citations for borrowed ideas, missing source information, unclear attribution, or unacknowledged AI use.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage; academic integrity expectations for graduate-level discussion writing.
  - ***Notes***: Minor errors may include APA punctuation, capitalization, or format inconsistency that does not block source tracing.


#### 3. EE for Citations and Attributions (100%)

*Uses citations for ideas requiring attribution, with few or no minor errors*


To exceed expectations (EE) for this guidance, the submission must meet all above requirements and address all of the following requirements:

- <a id="631"></a>6.3.1. Submission meets ME requirements, and 80% or more of citation and reference or source-information pairs follow APA 7 or another clearly consistent discipline-appropriate style closely.
  - **GO**: The student meets ME requirements and 80% or more of citation/source-information pairs follow APA 7 or another clearly consistent discipline-appropriate style closely.
  - **NOGO**: The student does not meet ME requirements, or style inconsistencies remain frequent enough that the attribution is not close to a consistent professional standard.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage; APA 7 expectations for graduate-level source attribution.
  - ***Notes***: This requirement rewards strong professional consistency, not perfection.
- <a id="632"></a>6.3.2. In-text citations, links, and reference or source-information entries are complete, consistent, and professionally presented.
  - **GO**: In-text citations, links, and reference or source-information entries are complete, consistent, and professionally presented.
  - **NOGO**: Attribution is usable but inconsistent, incomplete, or professionally uneven enough to fall short of EE.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage; APA 7 expectations for graduate-level source attribution.
  - ***Notes***: Discussion posts may use brief reference patterns or links, but the presentation should still be coherent and professional.
- <a id="633"></a>6.3.3. Source attribution is integrated smoothly into the discussion writing.
  - **GO**: Attribution is placed where it helps the reader understand what came from a source or AI-supported process without interrupting the flow of the discussion.
  - **NOGO**: Attribution is clustered unclearly, disconnected from the borrowed material, or inserted in a way that weakens readability.
  - **Source**: G&R, Directions, Initial Post; G&R, AI Usage.
  - ***Notes***: This requirement recognizes that strong attribution also improves communication quality.

## C. Glossary of Terms

### Borrowed idea
A **borrowed idea** is any idea, claim, fact, example, wording, paraphrase, quotation, source-derived insight, AI-generated suggestion, or other content that came from outside the student’s own original thinking and therefore requires citation, acknowledgement, or both.

### Graduate-level attempt

A **graduate-level attempt** is a discernible effort to address the criterion using reasoning, examples, terminology, reflection, or source use appropriate for graduate study in computer science and AI ethics. The attempt may contain errors, omissions, incomplete reasoning, or uneven writing, but it must show meaningful engagement with the discussion task beyond a superficial, generic, or unrelated response.

For checklist purposes, a graduate-level attempt should usually include at least one of the following:

- a clear effort to address the actual discussion prompt rather than only greet classmates or restate the prompt;
- discussion of AI tools, deep learning technologies, ethical concerns, human responsibility, or related course concepts;
- reasons, examples, or evidence that support a conclusion or response;
- visible engagement with a peer’s ideas in a response post.

A submission does **NOT** show a graduate-level attempt when it is blank, unrelated to the discussion, limited to generic filler, or lacks meaningful observable work toward the requirement.

### Error, Major in Citations
A **major error** in citations or attributions is a problem that prevents the reader from finding the source, understanding what information came from the source, or verifying that borrowed material or AI support was acknowledged appropriately. Major errors include missing citations for borrowed ideas, missing source information, unclear attribution, broken or incomplete links when no other retrieval information is given, or unacknowledged AI use when acknowledgement is required.

### Error, Minor in Citations
A **minor error** in citations or attributions is a problem that does not prevent the reader from finding the source or understanding the attribution. Minor errors usually involve APA 7 punctuation, capitalization, italics, ordering, hyperlink format, or other style-level inconsistencies.

- **Consistent minor errors**: More than 20% of the citations, links, or source-information entries contain minor errors, but the attribution pattern is still clear and usable.
- **Few or no minor errors**: Less than 20% of the citations, links, or source-information entries contain minor errors, and the attribution pattern is clear, consistent, and professionally presented.

### Initial post
The **initial post** is the student’s original contribution to the discussion thread. In this activity, it should be 1–2 paragraphs, include a brief self-introduction, identify AI tools or deep learning technologies the student uses, explain how AI has influenced the student’s field of study, and discuss the ethical concern or concerns the student sees as most critical.

### Response post
A **response post** is a reply to a classmate outside of the student’s own initial-post thread. In this activity, the required response posts should engage peer ideas about how ethical concerns may evolve as AI advances and what human responsibility in AI development requires.

### Evidence
In this checklist, **evidence** may include concrete examples, personal or professional experience, course concepts, peer ideas, cited sources, or observable real-world cases that help justify a conclusion rather than leaving it as unsupported assertion.
